Welcome to Last Modified Timestamp

Track when web pages were published or last updated with this simple, powerful extension.

Why We Built This Extension

Don't you hate it when you're on a webpage and you're unsure if the information is new or old?

Sadly, few websites clearly display when their content was published or last updated. This can be frustrating when you need to know if you're looking at current information.

Last Modified Timestamp solves this problem by finding both the **published** and **last modified** dates of a page. Simply click the extension icon to find out, giving you confidence in the content you're reading.

For the best experience, we recommend pinning this extension to your toolbar for easy access.
1
Click the extensions menu in Chrome

Find the puzzle icon in the top-right corner of your browser.

The extensions menu is where you can access all your installed Chrome extensions.
2
Pin Last Modified Timestamp to your toolbar

Click the pin icon next to our extension to keep it visible at all times.

Pinning gives you quick access to the extension's features from any webpage.
3
You're all set!

To find the timestamp for a page, just click the extension icon. The extension finds both "published" and "modified" dates, and you can customize the date/time format in the options.

Customize Your Experience

You can set your preferred date and time format. Right-click the extension icon, select "Options", and choose the format that works best for you.

How the Extension Works

We intelligently scan the webpage for both **published** and **modified** timestamps, checking various locations where a date might be hiding. Our approach follows a logical sequence, prioritizing the most reliable sources first:

1

Meta Tags

We start by looking for direct indicators in the page's meta tags. This includes standard tags like last-modified as well as Open Graph (OG) tags like og:updated_time (for modified) and og:published_time (for published).

2

Schema.org Structured Data

Next, we analyze any Schema.org structured data (in JSON-LD or microdata format). This often contains accurate datePublished and dateModified properties.

3

HTML Content Patterns

We search for HTML5 <time> elements and common phrases or CSS classes (like .post-date or .updated) that often indicate dates.

4

HTTP Last-Modified Header

As a final fallback for the modified date, we examine the Last-Modified header sent by the web server.

Note: Some webpages simply don't provide timestamp information in any form. In these cases, even our thorough checking can't find what doesn't exist.
